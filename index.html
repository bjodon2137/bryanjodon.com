Linework & Basic Shapes

0%
Fig. 1 — Line Study & Form ExercisesLesson 1
Goals
  • ◆Practice consistent line control
  • ◆Understand and draw basic geometric shapes
  • ◆Learn to combine shapes to form objects
4-Week Plan
Week 1
Pencil Control & Mark-Making
Learn pencil grips, pressure, and basic mark-making exercises.
Week 2
Lines & Exercises
Practice straight lines, curved lines, ghosted lines, and ellipses.
Week 3
Basic Shapes
Draw boxes, circles, triangles, and cylinders from observation and imagination.
Week 4
Combining Shapes
Break down objects into basic shapes. Draw from reference using shape analysis.
Key Concepts
Line Quality
The character of a line determined by pressure, speed, and confidence. Clean, purposeful lines communicate skill before anything else.
↓ Download
Ellipses & Circles
Circular forms in perspective become ellipses. Drawing smooth, symmetrical ellipses is foundational for cylinders, wheels, and cups.
↓ Download
Hatching & Texture Marks
Parallel or cross-directional lines used to build tone and texture. The foundation of shading with pen or pencil.
↓ Download
Basic Geometric Shapes
Triangle, square, circle — the building blocks of all complex forms. Every object can be broken down into these primitives.
↓ Download
Confident Marks
Drawing with intention rather than timid, overworked lines. Ghost the motion before committing — commit fully when you draw.
↓ Download
Organic vs Geometric Lines
Geometric lines are precise; organic lines are free and irregular. Knowing when to use each defines your drawing vocabulary.

Fig. 2 — Interior Corner & Box StudiesLesson 2
Goals
  • ◆Get an overall view of what perspective is (and isn't)
  • ◆Understand what makes simple shapes look 3D
  • ◆Train the eye to break the world down into boxes
  • ◆Understand and practice 1-point perspective
  • ◆Understand and practice 2-point perspective
  • ◆Draw 250 boxes (the 250 Box Challenge)
  • ◆Practice breaking objects down into simple forms
4-Week Plan
Week 1
Perspective Foundations
Understand what perspective actually is — horizon lines, vanishing points, 1-point and 2-point perspective.
Week 2
Boxes & Their Importance
Learn why boxes are the foundation of all form. Intro to 3-point perspective and drawing boxes from imagination.
Week 3
250 Box Challenge
Draw boxes. A lot of boxes. Work through the Draw a Box 250 Box Challenge to build spatial intuition.
Week 4
Beyond Boxes
Move from boxes to spheres, cylinders, and complex forms. Break real objects into simple 3D primitives.
Key Concepts
One-Point Perspective
All lines converge to a single vanishing point on the horizon. Used for head-on views of rooms, corridors, and streets.
↓ Download
Two-Point Perspective
Two vanishing points on the horizon line. The most useful setup for drawing objects and environments viewed at an angle.
↓ Download
Three-Point Perspective
A third vanishing point above or below adds vertical convergence — essential for dramatic angles and tall buildings.
↓ Download
Horizon Line & Eye Level
The horizon is always at the viewer's eye level. Everything above it is seen from below; everything below, from above.
↓ Download
Form & Volume
The illusion of 3D mass on a 2D surface — achieved through overlapping shapes, line weight variation, and shading.
↓ Download
Foreshortening
Objects angled toward the viewer appear compressed. A crucial skill for dynamic poses and dramatic compositions.
↓ Download

Fig. 3 — Proportion Studies & Measuring MethodsLesson 3
Goals
  • ◆Understand and apply various techniques for drawing accurate proportions
  • ◆Practice drawing objects with a grid and with the sight-size method
  • ◆Study more complex objects with correct proportions
Key Concepts
Comparative Measuring
Using one part of the subject as a unit to measure others. Proportional relationships, plumb lines, and finding reference heights.
↓ Download
Angles & Verticals
Transferring reference to paper by finding correct angles and using straight lines to build the initial structure.
↓ Download
Simple Shapes Method
See the simple shapes that compose a subject and transfer those to the page. For complex subjects, place them inside bigger bounding shapes first.
↓ Download
Negative Space
Check the shapes surrounding or in between the object — often easier to see than the object itself and a great accuracy check.
↓ Download
Hybrid Method
Block in visually first (train the eye to find shapes), then use structural measuring to make corrections. The best of both worlds.
↓ Download
Block-In & Straight Lines
Use straight lines to carve out the initial structure before adding curves. Easier to judge proportions with straights than curves.
↓ Download

Light & Shadow

Fig. 4 — Sphere, Cube & Cylinder: Chiaroscuro StudyLesson 4
Goals
  • ◆Create value scales and practice shading basic forms (spheres, cubes, cylinders)
  • ◆Draw objects with a single light source, experimenting with different angles
  • ◆Study light behavior on complex objects and natural forms
Key Concepts
Form Shadow vs Cast Shadow
Form shadow is on the object where light doesn't reach; cast shadow is thrown onto another surface. This distinction is fundamental.
↓ Download
The Terminator
The soft boundary between light and shadow on a rounded form. Its hardness or softness reveals the type and direction of the light source.
↓ Download
Five Elements of Shading
The classic framework: highlight, light, shadow, reflected light, and cast shadow. Every lit object displays all five elements.
↓ Download
Light Logic
Understanding how light behaves consistently — direction, intensity, and how it wraps around forms — ensures convincing shadows.
↓ Download
Value Structure
The overall arrangement of lights and darks in a composition. Good value structure reads clearly even as a small thumbnail.
↓ Download
Occlusion Shadow
The darkest, softest shadows in crevices and contact points where ambient light can't reach. Essential for grounding objects.

Key Concepts
Rendering Techniques
The range of approaches — hatching, blending, stippling, scumbling — used to build tone, texture, and surface quality.
↓ Download
Smooth Gradients
Gradual tonal transitions that describe curved surfaces and soft lighting. Achieved through controlled blending or fine hatching density.
↓ Download
Surface Texture
The visual quality of a material's surface — rough, smooth, porous, reflective. Rendered through mark-making that mimics its physical properties.
↓ Download
Stippling
Building tone using dots rather than lines. Highly controlled and time-intensive, but excellent for organic and detailed textures.
↓ Download
Implied Texture
Suggesting texture without drawing every detail — a few well-placed marks trigger the viewer's pattern recognition to fill in the rest.
↓ Download
Value & Contrast Control
Managing the lightest lights and darkest darks intentionally to guide the eye and create visual hierarchy in the image.

Fig. 6 — Color Wheel, Harmonies & TemperatureLesson 6
Goals
  • ◆Understand basic color theory and application
  • ◆Experiment with complementary and analogous color schemes
  • ◆Create color wheels and do color studies
Key Concepts
Hue, Saturation & Value
The three properties of every color. Hue is the name (red, blue), saturation is intensity, and value is lightness or darkness.
↓ Download
Color Temperature
Colors lean warm (red, orange, yellow) or cool (blue, purple, green). Warm light creates cool shadows and vice versa.
↓ Download
Complementary Colors
Colors opposite each other on the color wheel. Side by side they vibrate with energy; mixed together they neutralize to grey.
↓ Download
Color Mixing
How pigments combine to create new colors. Understanding primary, secondary, and tertiary mixing prevents muddy, lifeless color.
↓ Download
Local Color vs Light Color
Local color is a surface's actual color; light color is the hue cast by the light source. Seeing both is essential for naturalistic painting.
↓ Download
Color Harmony
Relationships between colors that create visual unity — analogous, complementary, triadic, and split-complementary schemes.
↓ Download
