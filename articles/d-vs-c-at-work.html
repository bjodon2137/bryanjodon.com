D vs. C: Why Two High-Performing Styles Create So Much Friction

D-types want to move fast and decide now. C-types want to get it right and need more information first. Both are reasonable. Neither is wrong. And yet they clash constantly.

If you've ever watched a D-type and a C-type try to reach a decision together, you've seen a particular kind of exhaustion set in. The D-type is ready to move the moment there's a reasonable direction. The C-type is still asking questions that, from the D's perspective, should have been answered two meetings ago. From the C's perspective, the D is about to make a decision they'll have to undo.

Both of them are operating from legitimate concerns. The tension between them isn't personality weakness on either side — it's a fundamental difference in what each style values most.

What each style is actually optimizing for

D — Dominance

Optimization target: Speed and results. D-types believe that a decision made now and corrected later is usually better than the perfect decision made too slowly. They have a high tolerance for imperfection in service of momentum.

They're not being reckless — they've calculated the risk and decided the cost of delay is higher than the cost of being wrong. They just haven't explained that calculation, because to them it's obvious.

C — Conscientiousness

Optimization target: Accuracy and quality. C-types believe that doing something right the first time is almost always worth the extra time it takes. They have a low tolerance for imprecision — not from perfectionism for its own sake, but because they've seen what downstream errors cost.

They're not being slow — they're preventing a problem the D hasn't modeled. They just haven't communicated that framing, because to them it's also obvious.

The friction comes from the fact that neither style makes their reasoning visible. The D assumes everyone else has the same urgency calculus. The C assumes everyone else values thoroughness as much as they do. They're talking past each other about the decision when the real disagreement is about the process.

The translation problem

Every style has a way of reading other styles that gets filtered through their own values. D-types and C-types are particularly prone to misreading each other because their core values sit at opposite ends of the urgency-accuracy spectrum.

"They're questioning my judgment instead of just executing."

Neither reading is accurate. But without a shared language for style differences, both become conclusions about character: the D is reckless, the C is obstinate. Once those conclusions form, the dynamic locks in and gets worse.

What actually helps

The D doesn't need to slow down. The C doesn't need to speed up. They need to understand what the other person is actually protecting — and build a process that honors both.

For D-types working with a C: give them a defined scope for their questions. "I need your analysis by Thursday, focused on these three risk areas." You're not asking them to skip rigor — you're channeling it. C-types thrive with constraints; open-ended "let me know what you think" is actually harder for them than a specific brief.

For C-types working with a D: lead with your conclusion, not your reasoning. The D can always ask for more depth. But if you open with a 10-minute explanation before you get to the point, you've lost them before you've made your case. Put your recommendation first. Then offer the data if they want it.

For both: be explicit about the decision-making process, not just the decision. "Here's how we're going to reach this decision, here's what inputs we need, and here's when we're committing" removes most of the friction before it starts. The D knows the process has an end. The C knows the process is real.

The D-C friction is one of the most common and most productive in high-performing teams — when it's understood. These two styles often catch each other's blind spots in ways that produce better outcomes than either would alone. The goal isn't to eliminate the tension. It's to stop wasting it.
